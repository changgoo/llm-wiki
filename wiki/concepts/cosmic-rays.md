# Cosmic Rays

## Definition

Cosmic rays are relativistic charged particles originating outside the Solar System. Most are protons and alpha particles, and their interactions with gas, magnetic fields, and plasma waves shape both their propagation and their astrophysical effects.

## Why It Matters

In galaxy formation, cosmic rays matter because they can store energy for long periods, diffuse or stream through magnetized plasma, and transfer momentum and heat to gas. [[ruszkowski-pfrommer-2023-cosmic-ray-feedback]] emphasizes that these properties make cosmic rays potentially important for star formation regulation, [[galactic-winds]], circumgalactic pressure support, and [[agn-feedback]].

## Supporting Evidence

- The Milky Way interstellar medium has cosmic ray energy density near equipartition with thermal, magnetic, and turbulent energy densities.
- Cosmic rays are confined in the Galactic disk far longer than a simple light-crossing time, allowing cosmic ray pressure to build up dynamically.
- Cosmic rays interact with plasma through scattering, streaming instabilities, collisions, and wave damping.

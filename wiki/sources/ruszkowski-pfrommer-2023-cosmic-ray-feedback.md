# Ruszkowski And Pfrommer 2023 Cosmic Ray Feedback

## Summary

Mateusz Ruszkowski and Christoph Pfrommer review cosmic ray feedback across galaxies, the circumgalactic medium, and galaxy clusters. The paper argues that cosmic rays can matter dynamically because they cool slowly compared with thermal gas and couple to plasma through wave-particle interactions, scattering, streaming, collisions, and wave damping. This makes them candidates for driving galactic winds, supporting or heating circumgalactic gas, and contributing to active galactic nucleus feedback in cluster cores.

The review is both pedagogical and synthetic. It first develops the plasma physics of cosmic ray acceleration, transport, instabilities, and losses, then applies those processes to astrophysical systems from molecular clouds and supernova-driven interstellar media to cluster-scale active galactic nucleus feedback. It ends by emphasizing that cosmic ray transport remains the central uncertainty: small-scale plasma scattering physics can control whether cosmic rays escape, build pressure, heat gas, or produce observable non-thermal emission.

## Key Claims

- Cosmic rays may be crucial to galaxy formation and evolution because their long cooling times and coupling to gas allow them to transfer momentum and heat across large scales. See [[cosmic-ray-feedback]].
- In the Milky Way interstellar medium, cosmic ray energy density is close to equipartition with thermal, magnetic, and turbulent energy densities, suggesting that cosmic rays can regulate star formation and gas dynamics.
- Cosmic ray pressure gradients can help drive large-scale galactic winds, especially if transport allows cosmic rays to remain coupled enough to accelerate gas without losing too much energy. See [[galactic-winds]].
- Cosmic rays transported into the circumgalactic medium can affect pressure support, thermal instability, precipitation, X-ray emission, gamma-ray constraints, and metal-line diagnostics. See [[circumgalactic-medium]].
- In galaxy groups and clusters, cosmic rays accelerated by active galactic nucleus jets may help solve the cooling flow problem by heating the intracluster medium through wave excitation and damping. See [[agn-feedback]].
- A complete predictive model depends on better cosmic ray transport theory, including scattering, streaming, diffusion, wave damping, turbulence, and spectral transport. See [[cosmic-ray-transport]].

## Structure

- Section 1 introduces cosmic rays, their discovery, basic properties, and role in galaxy evolution.
- Section 2 covers plasma physics: wave-particle interactions, acceleration, spatial transport, spectral transport, and cooling processes.
- Section 3 applies the physics to astrophysical systems: ionization, galactic winds, cosmological simulations, thermal instability, and massive hot halos.
- Section 4 reviews observational signatures: Milky Way propagation, outflows, non-thermal emission, circumgalactic diagnostics, cluster radio and gamma-ray constraints, and future observatories.
- Section 5 lists open questions in plasma physics, cosmic ray transport, galactic winds, circumgalactic gas, active galactic nucleus jet feedback, and reionization.

## Notable Details

- The review distinguishes low-energy cosmic rays, which are important for ionization and chemistry, from GeV and higher-energy cosmic rays, which are more important for dynamical feedback.
- It frames cosmic ray feedback as a multi-scale problem: solar-system-scale magnetic fluctuations can affect kiloparsec-scale winds and galaxy-scale baryon cycling.
- The paper treats observations as constraints on transport models, especially radio emission, gamma-ray emission, X-ray properties, molecular/metal-line diagnostics, and multi-messenger measurements.
- It identifies the lack of a self-consistent cosmic ray transport theory as a key barrier to reliable simulation predictions.

## Tensions And Contradictions

- Slow cosmic ray transport can build pressure gradients and help drive winds, but it can also increase inelastic losses and overproduce gamma rays.
- Very fast cosmic ray transport can avoid some loss and emission constraints, but cosmic rays may escape too quickly to drive winds efficiently.
- Cosmic ray heating in cluster cores is plausible, but the relative importance of cosmic ray heating versus turbulent mixing, shocks, and sound-wave dissipation remains unresolved.
- Existing phenomenological transport models can match some observed cosmic ray spectra, but current microphysical theories do not yet self-consistently reproduce the full set of observations.

## Open Questions

- What microphysical scattering processes determine effective cosmic ray transport in the multiphase interstellar medium?
- How do cosmic rays migrate from suppressed-transport regions near sources into faster large-scale transport regimes?
- What transport speeds maximize wind launching while remaining compatible with gamma-ray constraints?
- How much cosmic ray pressure support exists in the circumgalactic medium?
- Can active galactic nucleus injected cosmic rays offset cooling in cluster cores while satisfying radio, millimeter, optical, X-ray, and gamma-ray constraints?
- How should future simulations couple cosmic ray hydrodynamics, spectral transport, magnetohydrodynamics, and radiation hydrodynamics?

                                                                             Abstract
              Spiral arms greatly affect gas пђВows and star formation in disk galaxies. We use local three-dimensional simulations
              of the vertically stratiпђБed, self-gravitating, differentially rotating interstellar medium (ISM) subject to a stellar
              spiral potential to study the effects of spiral arms on star formation and formation of arm spurs/feathers. We adopt
              the TIGRESS framework of Kim & Ostriker to handle radiative heating and cooling, star formation, and ensuing
              supernova (SN) feedback. We пђБnd that more than 90% of star formation takes place in spiral arms, but the global
              star formation rate (SFR) in models with spiral arms is enhanced by less than a factor of 2 compared to the no-arm
              counterpart. This results from a quasi-linear relationship between the SFR surface density SSFR and the gas surface
              density ќ£, and supports the picture that spiral arms do not trigger star formation but rather concentrate star-forming
              regions. Correlated SN feedback produces gaseous spurs/feathers downstream from arms in both magnetized and
              unmagnetized models. These spurs/feathers are short lived and have magnetic пђБelds parallel to their length, in
              contrast to the longer-lived features with perpendicular magnetic пђБelds induced by gravitational instability. SN
              feedback drives the turbulent component of magnetic пђБelds, with the total magnetic пђБeld strength sublinearly
              proportional to ќ£. The total midplane pressure varies by a factor of вИЉ10 between arm and interarm regions but
              agrees locally with the total vertical ISM weight, while SSFR is locally consistent with the prediction of pressure-
              regulated, feedback-modulated theory.
